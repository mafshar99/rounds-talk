The stakes

Diagnostic error is the target.

A leading cause of preventable harm in the ICU. Roughly 1 in 5 hospitalized patients who die or are transferred to intensive care has a diagnostic error — and autopsy studies have long flagged missed diagnoses in a sizeable share of ICU deaths.
Limited trainingnarrow exposure to the long tail of critical illness
Incomplete data accessdiagnostic signal scattered across systems
Delayed recognitionserious symptoms surface late under acuity
Cognitive overloadthe sheer volume of EHR documentation
Under load, providers fall back on biased decision shortcuts — missing chances at timely, accurate diagnoses. At UW–Madison we are running a multicenter trial on diagnostic errors during clinical deterioration requiring ICU transfer; early results show high error rates.

How clinicians — and models — think

Fast thinking, slow thinking

Kahneman’s two systems explain both human diagnostic error and where AI fits. System 1 is fast and intuitive; System 2 is slow, analytic, and abstract.
System 1 · fast

Pattern & recall

Automatic, low-effort, intuitive — and where heuristics and bias quietly drive diagnostic error.

LLMs already excel here · extraction · retrieval · translation · summarization
System 2 · slow

Abstraction & analysis

Deliberate and effortful — weighing evidence, revising hypotheses, reasoning under uncertainty.

The historical AI gap · now reachable with reasoning models & agents
Pre-test probability→ System 1 · pattern System 2 · analysis→ Post-test probability→ Diagnosis
Both systems feed one Bayesian update — a pre-test probability revised toward a post-test probability. Errors enter when the prior is anchored, the base rate ignored, or the update miscalibrated. Diagnosis is largely a System 2 task — where reasoning models and agents are most relevant.
The human baseline

Even experts miss the odds

A national survey of 553 clinicians estimated disease probability against known reference standards. The pattern is consistent base-rate neglect — System 1 error in the open.
True reference range Clinicians (median) GPT-4 (median)
After a positive mammogram the true cancer risk is 3–9%; clinicians guessed 50%, GPT-4 8%. GPT-4 had less error after a negative result in all 5 cases — but the “UTI” case was really asymptomatic bacteriuria, and the model took the framing at face value. It inherits whatever prior the prompt hands it.

Uncertainty estimation

A calibrated probability, with its rationale

LLM generation is deterministic: it returns a single answer with no native estimate of how likely it is. Diagnosis is probabilistic and revised as evidence accrues, so the model should report a calibrated probability alongside the rationale behind it.
Classifier-only fine-tuning degrades generation
Parsable-output rate holds, then drops sharply past ~epoch 11 — generation degenerates to repeated tokens (“linguistic collapse”).
CLSGen — shared model, two heads
A single joint loss keeps the probability and the rationale aligned (0.95–0.98).

Why teaming is the hard part

A capable model is not a capable team.

A diagnostic LLM matched — even beat — physicians on its own. Yet handing physicians that same LLM didn’t significantly improve their diagnostic reasoning over usual resources.
On management reasoning, augmentation did help (+6.5%) — but physician + LLM was no better than the LLM alone. The human added little to the machine.
And it can backfire: one confidently-wrong suggestion dropped AI-trained physicians from 84.9% → 73.3%. Automation bias persists, even with training.
Team performance turns on how roles, communication, and shared understanding are structured — not raw model capability. That is the design problem Orchestra-ICU is built around: sourced drafts, human-in-the-loop, the clinician stays in control.

Where the day is decided

A day on ICU rounds

Daily rounds are the center of the ICU day — the team converges on each patient, the day’s decisions are made, and both the note and the orders flow from that conversation.
It starts in the conference room with radiology, then moves to the bedside — where Orchestra-ICU listens.
The progress note is started on rounds and carries the summarized discussion forward; orders are placed from the decisions. Much of the reasoning behind both lives only in the spoken conversation — never reaching the chart.
At the bedside · under IRB

Our track record with agents

MoMA: specialize, then aggregate

Specialize → aggregate → verify against the source → predict.
MoMA beat monolithic and fusion baselines — macro-F1 ≈ 0.85 on chest-trauma severity, consistent across sex & race. In one case the imaging specialist excluded severe findings and corrected a text-only model that called it severe → moderate ✓. The same decomposition Orchestra-ICU runs at the bedside.
How it all connects

Orchestra-ICU, end to end

Voice in at the bedside → FastAPI backend → a reasoning orchestrator plans and calls a fleet of specialized agents through MCP — patient-data agents pull the live chart, QC/QI agents check the work — one sourced draft out → the clinician decides. Every step traced, costed, and monitored.
The product · what the clinician sees

From sign-in to draft note

Connect to Epic over FHIR → ambient, hands-free rounds anyone on the team can query → Done Rounding generates a draft ICU progress note with a labeled guideline-based recommendations section from approved institutional pathways. The clinician reviews, edits, regenerates by section, and finalizes & exports — every section carries its provenance, and nothing is signed into the record. Researchers get a read-only mirror with secure export. Synthetic patient data; identifiers redacted.

Agent teammate · the LLM-as-a-Judge

Honest evaluation, run as an agent

01
Distrust the headline.

A 76% "action accuracy" was matched by a trivial always-escalate baseline; an externally-validated Epic sepsis model missed 67%. No baseline, no claim.

02
Give the Judge a validated rubric.

PDSQI-9 — 9 attributes, ICC 0.867 (JAMIA 2025); it names the two failure modes that matter — Accurate ↔ hallucination, Thorough ↔ omission. Now shipping as a tool inside Epic.

03
Run it as an agent.

Our LLM-as-a-Judge agent applies PDSQI-9 at scale: ICC 0.818 vs. humans — 22s vs. 600s, $0.05 vs. $50. Reasoning models judge better.

04
More agents ≠ better judge.

A multi-agent jury scored 0.768 — it did not beat the single judge. Rubric quality, not crowd size, decides.
